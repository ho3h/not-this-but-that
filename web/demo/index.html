There's a sentence I want you to read out loud

"This isn't a setback, it's a springboard."

You know that sentence. You've read it five hundred times this year. You couldn't pick the chatbot that wrote it out of a lineup of chatbots, because every chatbot writes that sentence. Different topic, same shape. It isn't X — it's Y. I'll call it the AI-ism for the rest of this piece, because that's what it is. The bot's tic. The deep tell.

I wanted to know if I could turn it off.

Not from the prompt side — that's been tried. You can ask the model not to do it; the model nods, then does it three sentences later. You can ban the word "just" from its vocabulary; the model finds a comma, finds an em-dash, finds another verb. The construction is too useful to the model. It is, on the inside of the model's head, doing something.

What I wanted to know is what that something was, and whether you could reach inside the model — open it up like a clock — and switch the something off. Most of the work in mechanistic interpretability looks for the one feature that runs a behaviour. Refusal, for instance, turned out to live in one direction in every model anyone's checked. Erase that direction, the model stops refusing to write your stalker fanfic.

I bet the AI-ism would be the same. I spent a weekend being wrong about that.

A digression into 1753

The construction is no chatbot invention — the rhetorical move is 2,800 years old, and it has a name.

In 1741 the Oxford Professor of Poetry, a thirty-one-year-old clergyman named Robert Lowth, started a series of Latin lectures on the literary structure of biblical Hebrew. Published in 1753 as Praelectiones de sacra poesi Hebraeorum. He gave the move a name: antithetic parallelism. Two clauses, structurally mirrored, one denied and one affirmed. "A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish son grief to his mother." The book of Proverbs is composed almost entirely of it.

Modern chatbots front-load the negation — "it's not X, it's Y" instead of "the wise A, but the foolish B" — but it's the same engine. Lowth was looking at it three millennia after it was invented. The chatbot has only had it for two years. The move itself is fine. What's wrong is the over-application — every chatbot reaches for it on every prompt, regardless of whether the antithesis actually clarifies anything.

The setup, in plain English

I'll move fast because the punchline is more interesting than the apparatus.

  1. The model. Google's Gemma 2 2B — an open instruction-tuned chatbot, two billion parameters, runs on a laptop. Small enough to probe directly, big enough to produce fluent prose. It produces the AI-ism in about seven and a half percent of replies on neutral prompts, which sounds low until you realise every other paragraph contains one.
  2. The microscope. A pre-trained sparse autoencoder (SAE) — a little learned dictionary that decomposes the model's internal state into a sum of 16,384 named features. Only a handful fire at any given moment. Each comes with an English-language label: feature 3223 is "phrases conveying exceptions or negations"; feature 9909 is "references to digital technology and online interactions"; and so on for the other 16,382. The labels are noisy but mostly truthful.
  3. The graph. All 16,384 features live in a Neo4j graph as nodes — edges between them encode which features write similar patterns (decoder cosine), which fire together (co-activation), and which automatic-cluster they belong to. Without the graph you can't reason about the features by hand — it's a 16k-element pile. via Neo4j
  4. The probe. Take a prompt the model is about to commit to the construction with — "It's not a tool" works. Truncate it right before the next word. Look at the probability the model assigns to any comma-and-pivot token that would complete the construction — ", but", ", it's", "—". Call that probability P(pivot). Baseline ≈ 0.28 across 80 prompts. That's the number to push down.
  5. The bet. Find the feature, or features, that drives P(pivot). Disable them. If the model stops committing, we've localised the construction. If it doesn't, we haven't.

The naïve attack

I did what anyone would do. I ranked all 16,384 features by how much zeroing them drops P(pivot). The winner, by a wide margin, was feature 3223. Label: phrases conveying exceptions or negations. You can imagine my satisfaction. The literal negation feature. Cinematic.

I zeroed it. P(pivot) dropped by 18%. Real drop, beat every random baseline.

So I went for the kill. I took feature 3223's direction in the model's internal vector space and projected it out of every layer — the method that erased refusal behaviour from a dozen chat models. It was supposed to make the construction structurally impossible. Cannot be represented anywhere. The model literally cannot write notes along that axis.

I ran the test. The first generation, on a prompt about an Antarctic research station, started:

"Imagine a world, not of green meadows and warm breezes, but of endless white, the sun a distant memory…"

The intervention designed to make the construction impossible opened with the construction.

I tried six more single-feature attacks. Different mechanisms. They all did the same partial thing: the most textbook form went away, and a different form often went up. The model wasn't being killed. The model was rerouting.

There's a name for this. It's called the Hydra Effect — knock out one path that produces a behaviour, another path compensates. The Hydra grows another head. Mostly studied on factual recall; here it was, in a rhetorical behaviour.

The realisation

Here's the lesson I should have started with.

The published refusal-direction work showed that one direction mediates refusal. That's a beautiful clean result. It made everyone in interp assume most behaviours work like that. Necessary and sufficient. One thing.

But there's no reason that has to be true. A behaviour can be necessary in one feature and not sufficient — the model needs the feature, and the model has other ways of getting the same effect when the feature is unavailable. That's what 3223 looks like. The model is, in some sense, hedging. The construction is implemented redundantly. A coalition of features cooperates to produce it, and the coalition is robust to losing any one member.

If that's the case, the right attack isn't a smarter way to disable one feature. The right attack is to find the rest of the coalition and disable all of them at once.

The eighth attack: kill the whole cluster

I ran the same per-feature attribution scan but this time kept the top hundred, not just the top one. Then I ablated them in joint-set ladders — top-2, top-5, top-10, top-25, top-50, top-100 — and measured P(pivot) at each rung.

set size P(pivot) drop relative
baseline 0.278 — —
1 (just 3223) 0.228 −0.050 −18%
2 0.174 −0.104 −37%
5 0.135 −0.143 −51%
10 0.107 −0.171 −61%
25 0.078 −0.200 −72%
50 0.069 −0.209 −75%
100 0.058 −0.220 −79%

There's the coalition. The first five features get you to 51%. The first ten get you to 61%. By twenty-five you've taken away 72% of the model's commitment to the construction at the decision point. By a hundred you asymptote at about 79%. After that the SAE basis at this layer has nothing more to give.

The control: ablating a hundred random features moves P(pivot) by under 0.001. The 79% drop is specific to this set rather than an artefact of mass ablation.

The model had only ever been a Hydra against my attempts to find one node at a time; hit from all twenty-five directions simultaneously, it loses three-quarters of its grip.

In actual prose, on actual prompts

The 79% above is at one token position. Does the kill survive in actual generation? Two tests — and a methodology footnote.

The footnote first. The construction has two surface forms the model uses interchangeably: "isn't X, it's Y" (same-sentence) and "isn't X. It's Y." (across sentences — the period-form). The strict regex+spaCy classifier this project inherited catches the first and misses the second. So every generation gets scored twice — strict + permissive — and I take the union. That's the honest "everything we can see" number. The strict-only numbers look bigger because they miss the model rerouting into period-form; the union is the truth.

One more tell about what was actually removed: the share of generations containing "but" collapses from 13.4% to 1.3%. What the coalition controls is the model's contrast machinery, and the AI-ism is what that machinery over-produces.

What the coalition actually looks like

I ran a leave-one-out from the top-25: for each feature, ablate the other twenty-four and see how much the drop falls. That tells you which features are individually indispensable (removing them costs a lot) versus individually substitutable (removing them costs almost nothing because the rest cover for them).

3223 "phrases conveying exceptions or negations" +0.073
9909 "references to digital technology and online interactions" +0.074
12898 "references to societal issues, particularly laws and marginalised groups" +0.021
11864 "technical terms and phrases related to legal and procedural contexts" +0.013
+21 more topical scaffolding — concern/caution, ethics, accountability, scientific measurement, urgency. Individually < 0.01 each. long tail

Two indispensable, one secondary, twenty-two substitutable. I extended the analysis to the top fifty features and the picture got cleaner: same two indispensables, same one secondary, and the twenty-five features ranked 26-50 all have cost-when-removed below 0.001. There's no second indispensable tier hiding past rank 25. The coalition really is two-cored.

The labels are the second surprise. I expected the coalition to be a cluster of "negation" features. It isn't. 3223 is about negation. 9909 is about digital tech. 12898 is about laws and marginalised groups. The rest are about concern, accountability, ethics, scientific measurement, urgency, professional roles. They look topical, not syntactic. My read: the construction commits when the negation feature plus the situational scaffolding around it is active. Contrastive correction lands when there's a thing to care about — a problem, a stake, a domain in which the contrast carries rhetorical weight.

The first surprise: the graph guessed wrong

The honest way to ask "which features are in the coalition?" would be structural: find features that look like 3223, or fire alongside it, or share a community. The graph hands you those queries cheaply. I ran them. All three failed.

prior N features P(pivot) drop added vs single 3223
3223 alone 1 −18% (reference)
3223 + 9 decoder-cosine neighbours 10 −20% +2pp
3223 + 9 top co-firing partners 10 −18% +0pp
3223's Leiden community top-10 10 −0.1% essentially zero
attribution top-10 (the actual coalition) 10 −61% +43pp

Structural similarity in the SAE doesn't predict causal-coalition membership for this behaviour. The features that do the construction with 3223 sit nowhere the graph expected — outside its decoder neighbours, outside its co-activators, outside its community-mates. They're a heterogeneous set the SAE never clustered, findable only by asking the causal question directly. Negative result — via Neo4j

This is what the graph was for. Three Cypher queries, three structural priors, an afternoon of measurement. The negative finding is itself a graph-derived discovery: a flat ranked list could never have framed the question as "is structural similarity the right prior?" because it has no structure.

The second surprise: it really is at one layer

Gemma 2 2B has twenty-six layers. The work above is all at layer 20. The natural follow-up: is layer 20 special? I re-ran the whole pipeline — attribution scan, ablation ladder — at layer 12 (early-middle) and layer 25 (late), each with its own top-25.

layer baseline P(pivot) after top-25 ablation relative drop
L12 (early) 0.288 0.202 −30%
L20 (middle) — the locus 0.278 0.077 −72%
L25 (late) 0.219 0.104 −52%

L12 is "the construction isn't built here yet". L25 has a real secondary coalition — but its baseline is lower because the L25 SAE itself loses ~19% of P(pivot) just by being inserted. The late-layer SAE basis is genuinely too narrow for the construction; the residual lives partly in its error term.

And the kicker: joint ablation across L12 + L20 + L25 simultaneously (75 features across three layers) reached the same absolute floor as L20 alone — 0.079 vs 0.077. The late-layer coalition turns out to be the same mechanism watched from downstream rather than an independent implementation. There's one place to intervene on the construction, and it's layer 20.

What's running on Neo4j?

The map of meanings is graph-backed

Every dot above is a :SAEFeature node in Neo4j. The graph holds what each feature means (its Neuronpedia label), what cluster it belongs to, and how it relates structurally to other features. The model performs the interventions; the graph chooses the targets and gives them names.

The graph powers

  • 16,384 :SAEFeature nodes with label, density, community id
  • 1.3M DECODER_SIMILAR edges (cosine of decoder columns)
  • 349k CO_ACTIVATES_WITH edges with PMI and Jaccard
  • Leiden communities for every feature (colours the regions)
  • Hypothesis-set generation — the structural priors above were single Cypher queries
  • Hover labels — every tooltip on the cloud is a LABELED_AS traversal

The model does

  • Forward passes through Gemma 2 2B-it
  • Ablation hooks at the L20 SAE post-encode
  • Sampling next tokens under intervention
  • Capturing per-token activations during generation

The pairing

A flat 16k-feature list is useless to navigate by hand. The graph turns it into a queryable map of meanings. The model turns the map into causal interventions. Together: an instrument.

Three demos built on the graph

The finding above — a coalition of twenty-five features causally implementing a behaviour — is interesting. What I want to convince you of here is a different claim: once that coalition is stored as a first-class object in a graph, with the rest of the SAE around it, you can do things you genuinely cannot do anywhere else.

Each of the three demos below is a Cypher query of fewer than twelve lines. Each is impossible without the graph.

Demo 1

Surgical de-slop — concept ∩ behaviour

The full coalition has twenty-five features. For any given prompt only a handful are topically relevant. The graph lets you intersect a prompt's semantic content — via vector search over the 16,384 SAE feature labels — with a named behaviour's feature set, and silence only the overlap.

For "Discuss the legal and medical implications of AI in healthcare" the graph returns two: feat 7361 (legal analysis and recommendations) and feat 1608 (legal and health-related terms). Of the twenty-five-feature coalition, those are the two your prompt actually invokes.

For "Explain quantum computing to a child" it returns zero — the AI-ism coalition isn't topically active for this prompt, so there's nothing to silence. The graph said leave it alone. 

MATCH (b:Behaviour {name: 'ai-ism'})-[r:INCLUDES]->(f:SAEFeature)
  WHERE f.index IN $retrieved AND f.sae_id CONTAINS 'L20/16k'
RETURN f.index AS idx, r.weight AS weight, r.rank AS rank
ORDER BY r.rank

Why this needs a graph: a vector DB can do the retrieve; only a graph with first-class behaviour membership can intersect it with a named coalition. RAG, but the retrieved objects are model features and the composition is an intervention, not a context window. Try it in the Playground →

Demo 2

Mix your own chatbot — weighted Cypher union

The playground has four :Behaviour nodes seeded: ai-ism (the coalition), bullets (bullet-point compulsion), hedging (caveats), formal_register (academic prose). Each is a twenty-five-feature subgraph, weighted by per-feature contribution.

The mixer gives you a slider per behaviour, 0-100%. The intensity controls how many of each behaviour's top features get pulled into the union. {ai-ism: 100, bullets: 50, hedging: 30} composes a 45-feature silence-set drawn from three behaviours at proportional depth. 

UNWIND $intensities AS i
MATCH (b:Behaviour {name: i.name})-[r:INCLUDES]->(f:SAEFeature)
  WITH b, f, r ORDER BY r.rank
  LIMIT toInteger(i.intensity * b.coalition_size / 100)
RETURN DISTINCT f.index AS idx, collect(b.name) AS sources

Why this needs a graph: the same query mechanism could mix personality_anxious, tone_dry, register_clinical — behaviours are user-defined, named, persistent, composable. Style as a graph algebra. The closest thing I've seen to a model personality control plane that's actually mechanistic — not a system prompt, not a fine-tune. Try the mixer →

Demo 3

Audit trail — feature provenance as graph paths

Every generation writes an :Intervention node with :USED_SOURCE edges to the things that selected each silenced feature — preset:top25, surgical_deslop:<prompt>, community_click:<region>, lasso, user_click, search:<query>. Each source is in turn :SELECTED → :SAEFeature.

So you can ask, weeks later: "Why did this run silence feature 3223?" The answer is one MATCH query. 

MATCH (i:Intervention {id: $iid})-[:USED_SOURCE]->(s:Source)-[:SELECTED]->(f:SAEFeature)
  OPTIONAL MATCH (f)-[:LABELED_AS {primary:true}]->(l:AutoInterpLabel)
RETURN s.kind, s.label, collect({idx: f.index, label: l.text}) AS features
ORDER BY s.ts

Why this needs a graph: the audit is a traversal rather than a join, across (intervention) → (source) → (feature) → (label) → (community). Path queries are what graphs are for. Glass-box governance — show me which features your AI silenced, and which lineage decided to silence them — wants this primitive. Inspect a run →

What only a graph can do here

  • First-class coalition membership — "these twenty-five features collectively implement that behaviour" as a queryable node with weighted edges. Demo 1's intersection has nothing to intersect with otherwise.
  • Composable behaviour subgraphs — Demo 2's union is one Cypher query over arbitrary named coalitions. Without typed edges between behaviours and features you'd rebuild the union in application code per request.
  • Lineage as paths — Demo 3's audit traverses (intervention) → (source) → (feature). That's a graph traversal, not a join.

Where I could be wrong

A friend with mech-interp credentials read a draft and pointed at four things. I'd rather pre-empt the hostile read.

  • This is post-hoc. The project's pre-registration committed to single-feature attacks; the coalition is what came out of those attacks failing. The hardest pre-registered control — matched-activation, where each coalition feature is paired with a same-activation-density non-coalition feature — was the next ask. I ran twenty independent matched draws. The coalition's drop was +0.200; the largest of the twenty matched-null drops was +0.001; the mean was slightly negative. The coalition beats all twenty draws. So the effect isn't an artefact of mass-ablating any twenty-five active features. Other controls (specificity tests on adversarial negation sets, interchange patching for sufficiency) haven't yet been done.
  • Sufficiency is asymmetric and I haven't fully retried it. Clamping the coalition's activations up doesn't reliably produce the construction in cases where the model wouldn't have used it. Necessity is clean; sufficiency is murky.
  • One model. One layer family. One width. Gemma 2 2B, Gemma Scope canonical SAEs at 16k width. Cross-model replication on Llama 3 or Mistral hasn't been done.
  • The coalition is a blunter scalpel than a single feature. Ablating just 3223 on held-out human prose leaves perplexity at 1.000× baseline. Ablating the full top-25 pushes it to 1.079×. Still inside the conventional "scalpel not sledgehammer" gate of <1.20×, but a meaningfully bigger perturbation. The de-slop claim should be read with that in mind — you're not getting the kill for free.
  • The strict classifier under-counts both ways. It misses the period-form. The post's numbers use the union of strict + permissive scoring; a reader who writes an even more permissive detector will probably get an even lower drop, and that's fine — it would mean the model also reroutes into shapes neither detector catches, which is its own interesting finding.

Coalition: top-25 by per-feature causal attribution to P(pivot) at truncated D1 prefixes (scripts/pivot_attribution.py → reports/pivot_attribution.json). All committed in src/classifier/.

Graph substrate: Neo4j on bolt://localhost:7693; schema in cypher/; ingestion via scripts/04_ingest_features.py. UMAP coordinates from scripts/build_demo_layout.py, which pulls community id and label from Neo4j for every node.
