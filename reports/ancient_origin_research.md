# Ancient-origin research — notes for the Medium post cold open

## The discovery

Robert Lowth (1710–1787), English bishop and Hebraist, gave a series of
34 Latin lectures at Oxford as Professor of Poetry between 1741 and the
publication of *Praelectiones de sacra poesi Hebraeorum* (Oxford, 1753).
English translation: *Lectures on the Sacred Poetry of the Hebrews*
(London, 1787).

Lowth was the first modern scholar to formulate the theory of
**parallelism** as the structural basis of biblical Hebrew poetry. He
identified three forms:

1. **Synonymous parallelism** — the second line restates the first
   ("The heavens declare the glory of God / and the firmament shows his
   handiwork" — Psalm 19:1).
2. **Antithetic parallelism** — the second line contrasts with the
   first ("A wise son brings joy to his father / but a foolish son
   grief to his mother" — Proverbs 10:1).
3. **Synthetic parallelism** — balance of expression without strict
   synonymy or antithesis (a catch-all category for non-mirrored
   couplets).

Direct quote from Lowth (Lecture XIX, on antithesis): antithetic
parallelism "gives an acuteness and force to adages and moral
sentences, and therefore abounds in Solomon's Proverbs, but elsewhere
is not often to be met with."

## Why this matters for the post

Antithetic parallelism — "X, but not-X" / "the wise A, the foolish B" —
is **the rhetorical ancestor of "not X, but Y"**. The negation just
moves from the second clause to the first:

- **Antithetic parallelism (Proverbs 10:1, ~10th century BCE):**
  "A wise son brings joy to his father, but a foolish son grief to
  his mother."
- **The same rhetorical move with negation fronted:**
  "It's not the wise son who grieves the mother, but the foolish one."

The modern AI-ism is the inverted, contraction-flavored descendant:
"It's not about X, it's about Y" / "Not just X, but Y."

The form is 2700+ years old. The mockable thing isn't that LLMs use it;
it's that they over-use it, and at low diversity. Which makes the
gauntlet's question — *can we surgically remove it without breaking the
prose around it?* — a more interesting question than "ban this slop."

## Citable sources

- Lowth, R. (1753). *De sacra poesi Hebraeorum praelectiones academicae
  Oxonii habitae*. Oxford: Clarendon. (Latin; English translation:
  Lowth, R., trans. Gregory, G., 1787, *Lectures on the Sacred Poetry
  of the Hebrews*, London: J. Johnson.)
- Berlin, A. (2008). *The Dynamics of Biblical Parallelism* (Eerdmans).
  The standard modern reanalysis.
- Kugel, J. (1981). *The Idea of Biblical Poetry: Parallelism and Its
  History*. Yale UP. The provocation that parallelism is on a
  continuum, not a typology — but Lowth's antithetic class survives.

## Proverbs examples in straightforward English

For the post, use these — they make the ancient-form ↔ modern-form
mapping immediate:

- **Proverbs 10:1** — "A wise son brings joy to his father, but a
  foolish son grief to his mother." (Antithetic: positive vs negative
  outcome.)
- **Proverbs 10:7** — "The memory of the righteous is a blessing, but
  the name of the wicked will rot." (Antithetic: praise vs decay.)
- **Proverbs 15:1** — "A soft answer turns away wrath, but a harsh
  word stirs up anger." (Antithetic: cause-effect inversion.)
- **Ecclesiastes 10:2** — "The heart of the wise inclines to the
  right, but the heart of the fool to the left." (Antithetic: spatial
  metaphor.)

(All quoted in standard English-language translations; specific
edition reference for the post: Robert Alter, *The Hebrew Bible: A
Translation with Commentary* (Norton, 2019), since Alter's literary
translations preserve the parallel structure more faithfully than
older ones.)

## Notes for the writing

- The audience for the Medium post is technical-curious but not
  necessarily biblical-literate. Don't open with "Hebrew poetry" — open
  with the joke (the AI-ism), then pivot to the surprise (it's ancient),
  then to the gauntlet (can we kill it without breaking the rest).
- Lowth wrote in Latin, lectured at Oxford, became Bishop of London.
  He's the *establishment* — which makes the rhetorical move he isolated
  feel even older than Bishops.
- The parallelism literature uses "the A-line" and "the B-line" for the
  two halves. Use those terms when needed; they're the standard.
- The modern AI-ism is one specific form (F1: contrastive correction).
  The post should mention that the gauntlet handles seven forms (F1-F7)
  but the headline beat is on the family as a whole.
